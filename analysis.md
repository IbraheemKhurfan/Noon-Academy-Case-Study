# Analysis

## Diagnosis

On Day 14, six days before Quiz 2, 199 of 200 students (99.5%) are below their target score, yet only 13.5% of students who need support have documented facilitator contact since Quiz 1 — the bottleneck is not detecting who is struggling, it is converting that into a completed, tracked intervention before time runs out. Students who look similar on one risk number need very different help: some show a sudden attendance collapse (the top-priority student's recent attendance fell 51 minutes/day versus their own baseline), others still attend but have nearly stopped practicing, and many are simply a few points under target with stable behavior. With 8 facilitators covering 20-38 students each, and a measured 0% successful-interaction rate before any facilitator has used the system, the job is to turn a 200-student list into a short, correctly-prioritized daily queue and prove, from tracked outcomes rather than generated recommendations, that coverage is moving toward 80%.

## What you found in the data

- 199 of 200 students (99.5%) are below their target score, and only 13.5% of students who need support have documented post-Quiz-1 activity — 173 students (86.5%) show the `NO_POST_QUIZ_INTERVENTION` pattern.
- 67 of 200 students (33.5%) failed Quiz 1 outright (below 60/100) — a distinct fact from being below target, since targets vary a lot per student (see "Failing score vs. target gap" below).
- Target scores vary sharply by track: Accelerated averages 92.3 (tight, std 1.4), Standard 81.0 (std 7.3), Remedial only 45.9 (std 19.5, range 12–95) — track composition is 128 Standard / 58 Remedial / 14 Accelerated, and a Remedial student's target is set to their own baseline, not a shared bar.
- 102 of 180 facilitator notes (57%) have a `facilitator_email` that does not match the student's assigned facilitator in `student_metadata`; these are retained but excluded from parent communication and qualitative risk scoring until trusted.
- A further 55 of the 180 notes (30.6%) name, in the note text itself, a different roster student than the one they're filed under, even though the facilitator_email matches — see "Note content vs. student mismatch" below for how this was investigated and handled.
- Facilitator workload is genuinely uneven: 8 facilitators cover between 20 and 38 students each — not normalized, since the clarification confirmed this imbalance is real operating data, not a data bug.
- Risk segmentation splits the below-target population into sharply different situations — 2 Critical, 45 High, 35 Medium, 118 Low — alongside 3 missing-attendance rows, 3 extreme practice-volume days, and 1 quiz score logged before Quiz 1's date, each a real anomaly rather than a hypothetical.

## Design decisions on ambiguous data

The brief and follow-up clarifications deliberately left several judgment calls to us. Here is what we decided and why, so the reasoning is auditable rather than assumed.

**Failing score vs. target gap.** A score below 60/100 is a failing grade in absolute terms; a score below a student's own target is underperformance relative to plan. These are different facts and we score them differently: `gap_to_target` drives a proportional score (`src/scoring.py::performance_risk`), but a genuinely failing score sets a *floor* on top of that (15–25 of the 25-point performance-risk budget, `FAILING_QUIZ_SCORE` in `src/patterns.py`) rather than adding on top of the gap score. We chose a floor, not a sum, so a student is never penalized twice for the same underlying fact. The floor matters in practice: among the 52 Remedial students who fail the absolute 60-point line, the median gap to their own (lower) target is only 6 points — on the gap formula alone that would barely register as a moderate concern, understating that they failed the quiz outright. The floor corrects that without inventing a new scoring axis.

**Does learning track affect priority?** No — track is never a scoring input (`src/scoring.py` docstring; verified by grep that no `score_*` function reads `learning_track`). It is used only to build peer percentiles (a Remedial student is compared against Remedial peers, not the whole cohort, in `features.add_peer_percentiles`), so a student is never penalized simply for being on that track. We considered scoring track directly (e.g. a flat Remedial risk bump) and rejected it: track and absolute quiz performance are already highly correlated in this data — 52 of 58 Remedial students (89.7%) trip the `FAILING_QUIZ_SCORE` floor, versus 15 of 128 Standard and 0 of 14 Accelerated — so a direct track bump would double-count the same signal the failing-score floor already captures, while a comfortably-performing Remedial student (the other 10%) would be penalized for their track label alone. Track composition is real and worth a human looking at (a school running 29% of its cohort on a remedial curriculum is a staffing/curriculum fact, not just a scoring input), but it belongs in the admin's data-quality/composition view, not in the per-student risk number.

**Note content vs. student mismatch.** Some facilitator notes describe a different student than the `student_id` they're filed under. We investigated by translating each roster student's first name into its common Arabic spellings (`src/config.py::ARABIC_NAME_VARIANTS`) and checking whether a note's Arabic text names *another* roster student's first name while never naming its own assigned student (`src/validation.py::_detect_content_mismatch`). Findings: 137 of 180 notes (76%) name a different student's first name at face value; 82 of those also already have a `facilitator_email` that doesn't match the assigned facilitator, suggesting a plausible shared cause (notes filed in bulk under the wrong student/facilitator pairing at the same time). The remaining 55 (30.6%) have matching facilitator ownership but still name a different student in the text — a genuine, separate ambiguity. We cannot tell whether the `student_id` field or the note's own text is the one that's wrong, so the system does not guess: it flags the note with a new `content_mismatch` trust status (distinct from `unverified_ownership`), keeps it visible to the facilitator (purple badge, explanatory caption in the Notes tab), but excludes it from LLM analysis, `trusted_note_risk` scoring, and parent-facing communication until a human confirms which student it actually concerns — the same trust-then-use pattern already applied to ownership mismatches. Two limitations are intentional: a note that never names anyone by name is invisible to this check (we'd rather under-flag than falsely accuse a normal note), and two different students sharing a first name cannot be told apart by a first-name-only heuristic (this roster does contain first+last name collisions, discovered separately while preparing demo data).

**A note is not automatically an intervention.** A note is a facilitator's recorded observation; an intervention is a tracked action (call, tutoring session, message). The system keeps these as separate tables (`FacilitatorNote` vs. `Intervention`) and never auto-creates one from the other — a note only ever feeds `trusted_note_risk` (as a severity signal, via LLM classification of *trusted* notes only) or shows up as supporting evidence on a student's Overview/Timeline. A facilitator who wants a note's observation to become a tracked action still explicitly logs or accepts an intervention for it.

**`days_until_next_quiz` anomalies.** The field counts down 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3 and then jumps back up to 10 partway through the period. This is not corrupted data — it is fully explained by the transition from Quiz 1 (2025-10-10) to Quiz 2 (2025-10-20), exactly 10 days apart, which matches the jump-to-10 value exactly. We derive both quiz dates from the data/config rather than hardcoding a naive linear countdown, which is why this pipeline handles the transition correctly instead of treating it as an anomaly to smooth over.

**Manual data entry.** Attendance and practice metrics are collected automatically by the platform — facilitators don't enter them day-to-day. The Data Entry page is framed accordingly: its manual-entry form and CSV upload are labeled and captioned as a correction/backfill tool for the specific case where the automatic feed missed or misrecorded a day, not as the normal path data takes into the system. Notes and roster edits remain genuinely facilitator-entered, so those keep their original framing.

## What you built and why

- A deterministic, component-based risk score (performance/engagement/trajectory/trusted-note/intervention-gap) instead of a black box, so every score is explainable and testable.
- Rule-based pattern detection (14 patterns) fully separated from LLM usage, so priority never silently shifts with a model update.
- A coverage funnel (recommendation → attempt → success → completed), not one "coverage" number — a recommendation or a no-answer call is not a completed intervention, and conflating them would hide the exact gap this study is about.
- LLM usage scoped to three language tasks (note understanding, messages, parent briefs), each with a deterministic fallback, so the pipeline runs identically with `LLM_ENABLED=false`.
- One Streamlit app with role-based views (facilitator "My Day" queue vs. admin oversight), so the daily action list — not a dashboard — is the primary surface.

## What you cut and why

No trained ML model: Quiz 2 outcome labels do not exist yet, so a black-box score would be less trustworthy than an auditable rule set for a facilitator acting on it today.
No uncontrolled automatic parent communication: 57% of notes carry an unverified facilitator/student pairing, and a further 30.6% carry a content-vs-student mismatch, so identity and contact data need human validation first — every notification here is a facilitator-reviewed dry run, never an automatic send.

## What you'd build next

The most valuable next step is closing the loop this system can only half-close today: recommended intervention → completed intervention → outcome → behavior change → future quiz result. The pipeline can measure whether a facilitator completed an action, but not whether that action worked — did attendance recover after a parent call, did practice resume after a check-in, did the eventual Quiz 2 score beat what the pre-intervention trajectory implied? Once Quiz 2 results land, each completed intervention becomes a labeled example (action type, before/after state, outcome), turning today's rule weights from an expert-authored guess into something empirically tunable, so the system can recommend whichever action type has actually worked best for a given pattern.
